What is redlining?

Redlining is the process through which de facto segregation was maintained after explicit segregation was made illegal, through a process of systematically maintaining majority-white neighborhoods while neglecting those of minorities, especially African Americans.

How did redlining work?

The following are some of the tactics and mechanisms of redlining:

  • Racialized "Residential Security" Maps
  • Denying mortgage applications in "undesirable" neighborhoods
  • Lending to low-income white people over middle-class minorities
  • Reduced home ownership
  • Peddling predatory loans
  • Encouraging white flight

While explicit redlining was made illegal through the 1968 Fair Housing Act and 1977 Community Reinvestment Act, its repercussions and offshoots are still felt today.

Continued effects of redlining

Redlining and its effects have been studied by many organizations, including those which have helpfully produced the following infographics. Click each image to see the complete picture.

Generational wealth

Perhaps the most sinister lingering symptom of redlining is the racial disparity in generational wealth. Due to difficulties in obtaining mortgages at reasonable prices, many Black families were forced to rent, spending their wages on enriching landlords instead of obtaining real estate which can be passed on to descendants. Since at least 1994, at least 70% of Non-Hispanic white householders have owned their homes, as opposed to less than half of Black households and under 55% of Hispanic households.

Redlining in Philadelphia

Philadelphia neighborhoods that were given HOLC's lowest D-rating include some of the city's most poverty-stricken areas such as North Philadelphia, as well as areas like Black Bottom and parts of East Passyunk which have been largely destroyed and replaced by University City and the Stadium District.

Effects on Education

This segregation continues to be propagated forwards, in large part due to its impacts on education. Since American public schools are primarily funded by property taxes, schools in wealthy areas have the highest budget for maintenance and teacher salaries, whereas poorer districts (frequently the victims of redlining) have to cut corners to make ends meet, resulting in crumbling buildings and out-of-date textbooks.
